Network Traffic & Privacy

Undergraduate Research Assistant, Department of Computer Science
PythonSQLLaTeXStatistical AnalysisIntrusion DetectionData Anonymization
The Problem

The lab was collecting network traffic data from 500+ users but had no good way to anonymize it before anyone looked at it. The process was manual, slow, and it meant raw data sat around longer than it should. Research timelines were slipping because of it.

What I Built

I wrote Python and SQL pipelines to automate the anonymization, replacing a recurring manual task entirely. I also ran benchmarks on several intrusion detection systems to see which ones actually held up on real network data. The goal was to give the team real numbers they could use to make decisions, not just theoretical comparisons.

Independent Research

Through the UR2PHD program (selected from 200+ applicants), I did my own independent security research. Found 3 vulnerabilities that hadn't been documented before, wrote up mitigation approaches in a formal proposal, and presented it to faculty. Not bad for a side project.

Reporting

I wrote the research papers and reports that came out of this work. The kind that faculty and researchers actually read and use, not just archive. Gave presentations to mixed audiences of researchers and faculty who had very different levels of technical context. Writing clearly about security stuff is harder than it sounds.

Results
  • Research turnaround 50% faster after the pipeline replaced manual anonymization.
  • IDS benchmark hit 95% accuracy, giving the lab a clear recommendation on which system to use.
  • Security findings made it into departmental guidelines. People actually changed how they work based on the writeup.
  • Identified 3 undocumented vulnerabilities with mitigation approaches proposed.

What I took away

Two years is a long time to spend on one project. What I got out of it was learning how to make research actually move. Fix the infrastructure, run the analysis carefully, then make the findings easy enough to act on that someone actually does.

The benchmark number is cool. But the guideline adoption is the thing I'd point to. That means the work landed.
